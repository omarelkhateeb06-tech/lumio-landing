## reading

A big task with a distant deadline does something cruel to your motivation. "Organize the company offsite" or "launch the new website by Q3" is too large to hold in your head, so you do not know where to start, so you do not start. The problem is not the work; it is that the work has not been broken down yet. AI is genuinely good at this kind of decomposition, turning a vague mountain into a numbered set of footholds.

Here is how to get a plan you can actually act on.

**Give it the goal, the deadline, and your reality.** "I need to plan and run a 40-person team offsite. It is in 10 weeks. I work on this about 3 hours a week and I have no events experience. Break this into a week-by-week plan with clear milestones." The constraints (your time, your experience, the date) are what make the plan realistic instead of a generic checklist.

**Ask for order and dependencies.** "What has to happen before what? Flag anything I should start early because it has a long lead time." This catches the classic planning trap, the thing you needed to book two months out that you discover too late.

**Request the first concrete step.** End with "What is the single first thing I should do this week?" A plan you cannot start is just a longer way of feeling stuck. One clear first action breaks the paralysis.

**Then apply what only you know.** This is the part the AI cannot do. It does not know that your venue contact is slow to reply, that the holiday week is dead, or that your boss wants to approve the agenda personally. Take its plan and adjust the timing and order against your real-world knowledge. The AI gives you the skeleton; you make it fit reality.

A plan from AI is a draft, not a command. But going from "this is overwhelming and I have not started" to "here are my weekly milestones and my first task is booking the venue" is often the entire difference between a project that drifts and one that gets done.

## mini_project

Take a real project you have been putting off and turn it into a timeline you can start this week. First, write down the project, the hard deadline, and your honest constraints: how much time per week you can give it and any relevant inexperience or dependencies. Second, ask AI to break it into an ordered, week-by-week plan with clear milestones, then ask a follow-up about dependencies and anything with a long lead time you should start early. Third, ask for the single first action to take this week. Finally, edit the plan against what you actually know: adjust any timing that is unrealistic, reorder steps based on real-world dependencies the AI could not know, and remove anything that does not apply. The deliverable is a dated milestone plan and one concrete first step, taking you from paralyzed to moving.
