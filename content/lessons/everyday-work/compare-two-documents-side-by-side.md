## reading

Comparing two documents is one of those tasks that is simple in theory and miserable in practice. Two versions of a contract, an old policy and a revised one, a draft and the edited return, your proposal and a competitor's. The differences are in there somewhere, but finding them by reading both line by line is slow, and it is exactly the kind of careful task where tired eyes miss the one change that mattered.

AI is excellent at this, because comparing text is squarely in its wheelhouse. Here is how to do it.

**Paste both, clearly labeled.** Give the AI both documents and mark which is which. "Here is version A, our current contract. Here is version B, the one they sent back. Compare them." The labeling keeps the AI from getting confused about direction.

**Tell it what you care about.** A raw "what changed" can return trivial differences alongside critical ones. Aim it: "Focus on changes to payment terms, deadlines, and liability. Ignore formatting and wording tweaks that do not change the meaning." Now you get signal, not noise.

**Ask for the changes grouped by importance.** "List the changes in three groups: critical (affects money, obligations, or risk), moderate, and minor." This turns a flat list into a triage you can act on, so you spend your attention on the clauses that matter.

**Ask what it means, not just what changed.** Follow up with "For each critical change, explain in plain language what it means for me and whether I should be concerned." That is the difference between knowing a clause moved and understanding why you should care.

One important caution: for anything legal, financial, or binding, AI's comparison is your fast first pass, not your final authority. It can miss a subtle change or misread a clause. Use it to find what to look at, then read those specific sections yourself, or have the right expert confirm them. The AI saves you from reading 20 pages; you still read the 3 that count.

## mini_project

Use AI to compare two real versions of a document you actually need to review. First, gather both versions (a current and a revised policy, two drafts of a proposal, an original and an edited document) and remove any confidential names or details if you are using a public tool. Second, paste both in clearly labeled as A and B, and ask the AI to list what changed, focusing only on the differences that affect meaning, not formatting. Third, ask it to group the changes by importance (critical, moderate, minor) and to explain in plain language what each critical change means for you. Finally, take the AI's list of critical changes and read those specific sections in the real document yourself to confirm them. The goal is to go from "I have to read both of these end to end" to "I know the three changes that matter and I verified them," in a fraction of the time.
