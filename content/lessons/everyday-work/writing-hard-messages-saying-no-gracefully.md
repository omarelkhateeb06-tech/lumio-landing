## reading

Some messages sit in your drafts for days. Not because they are long, but because they are hard: declining a request, telling a vendor their work missed the mark, pushing back on your boss, saying no to a favor you genuinely cannot do. You want to be honest without being harsh, and finding that balance under stress is exhausting. This is a place AI quietly earns its keep.

The trick is the same one that powers good email drafting, but the inputs matter more here because the stakes are personal.

Give the AI three things: **the situation, your actual position, and the relationship you want to keep.**

For example: "I need to decline a colleague's request to join their committee. I genuinely do not have the time and I am not willing to overcommit again. But I respect them and want to stay on good terms. Draft a short, warm message that says no clearly, gives a brief honest reason, and does not leave the door falsely open."

That last instruction matters. A common failure in hard messages is the soft no that sounds like a maybe, which only prolongs the discomfort for both people. Telling the AI to be clear, not wishy-washy, gets you a message that actually closes the loop.

A few moves that make hard messages land well:

**Name the tone explicitly.** "Warm but firm." "Apologetic but not groveling." "Direct without being cold." The tone is the whole game in a hard message, so do not leave it to chance.

**Ask for brevity.** Hard messages get worse the longer they ramble. "Keep it under 100 words" forces the AI to be clean and direct, which is exactly what these messages need.

**Keep the honesty yours.** The AI can find graceful wording, but you decide what is true and what you are willing to say. Read it and make sure it reflects your real position, not a smoother version you do not mean.

Then do the thing you always do: read it once, adjust the one detail only you know, and send. The message that haunted your drafts folder for three days goes out in three minutes, and it sounds like a thoughtful adult wrote it, because one did.
