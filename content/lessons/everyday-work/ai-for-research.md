## reading

When you need to get up to speed on something unfamiliar, a new regulation, a competitor, a medical term, a software tool, AI can save you hours. But research is also where AI is most dangerous if you trust it blindly, because it will state guesses with total confidence.

So use it for what it is great at, and verify what it is shaky on.

AI is excellent at orientation. It can take a topic you know nothing about and give you the lay of the land: the main concepts, the key terms, how the pieces fit together. "Explain how commercial leases typically work, in plain language, as if I am brand new to this. Cover the main types and the terms I should watch for."

It is also great at leveling and reframing. "Explain this like I am a complete beginner." "Now explain it like I am a manager who needs to make a decision." "What are the most common misconceptions about this?" Each angle deepens your understanding fast.

Where it gets risky is specifics. Exact statistics, current prices, recent events, named studies, legal citations: these are the things AI is most likely to invent. Anything called a hallucination tends to live in the details.

So the workflow is: use AI to understand the shape of a topic and learn the vocabulary, then take any specific fact you plan to rely on and confirm it against a real source, the official site, the actual document, or a trusted expert. Orientation from AI, verification from the world.

Done this way, AI does not replace research. It makes you a faster, better-oriented researcher who knows the right questions to ask.
