## reading

If you have started using AI well, you have probably hit the next wall: the colleague who rolls their eyes, the manager who is wary, the teammate who tried it once, got a bad answer, and wrote it off. You cannot move a workflow forward alone, so bringing skeptics along is a real skill, and it is mostly about people, not technology.

The mistake is leading with enthusiasm and features. "It's amazing, it can do anything" lands as hype and deepens the resistance. Skeptics are not won by excitement. They are won by a relevant result and by having their actual concern taken seriously.

**Name the real fear.** Most resistance is one of three worries: "it will replace me," "it will produce garbage I have to fix," or "I'll look like I'm cheating." Surface it honestly. "I'm not trying to replace your judgment, I'm trying to skip the blank-page part" disarms far more than a feature list.

**Show, on their task.** Do not demo a generic trick. Take a task they personally find tedious and show AI doing the boring 80 percent while they keep the judgment. A skeptic who watches AI turn their own messy notes into a clean draft in thirty seconds learns more than any pitch.

**Be honest about the limits.** Skeptics trust people who admit the downsides. Show where it gets things wrong and how you check it. Counterintuitively, acknowledging the flaws makes them trust the tool more, because you are clearly not selling them.

**Start them with one safe win.** Give them a single, low-stakes, high-annoyance task to try, with a prompt that already works. One real win they experienced themselves beats ten you described.

The goal is not to convert a believer overnight. It is to move someone from "this is nonsense" to "huh, that actually saved me time," on a task they care about, with their concern respected. That shift is what spreads through a team.
