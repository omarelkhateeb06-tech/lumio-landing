## reading

Here is the pattern almost everyone goes through. They learn AI can help, they get excited, they use it for a week, and then they drift back to doing everything the old way. Not because the tool let them down, but because reaching for it never became automatic. The hard part of AI is not the prompting. It is the remembering.

The skill in this lesson is workflow fit: getting AI to live inside the work you already do, instead of being a separate thing you have to remember to go visit.

**Attach it to an existing task, not a new one.** Do not resolve to "use AI more." Pick one task you already do on a schedule and bolt AI onto it. "Every Monday when I write the team update, I draft it with AI first." The existing habit carries the new one.

**Create a trigger.** A habit needs a cue. Keep the tool pinned in your browser, leave a saved prompt at the top of your weekly doc, or put a sticky note where the task happens. The goal is that the task itself reminds you, so you do not rely on willpower.

**Start with one or two, not ten.** People who try to use AI for everything at once use it for nothing within a month. Pick the one or two tasks where it clearly helps, let those become automatic, then add a third. Slow adoption is the kind that lasts.

**Lower the friction.** If using AI means opening a new tab, logging in, and retyping context every time, you will quit. Save your common prompts, set up custom instructions once, and keep the tool one click away. Every bit of friction you remove is a future day you actually use it.

The test of whether AI has stuck is simple: a month from now, are you still reaching for it without thinking? That only happens when it is wired into a real, recurring task, not floating as a good intention.

## mini_project

Wire AI into one real, recurring task this week so it becomes a habit instead of a novelty. First, pick a single task you already do on a predictable schedule (a weekly report, a recurring type of email, a standing meeting prep) and write down exactly when it happens. Second, design the trigger: decide where the reminder will live (a pinned tab, a saved prompt at the top of the relevant doc, a calendar note) so the task itself cues you to use AI. Third, write and save the specific prompt you will reuse for that task, with your context already baked in, so there is no friction next time. Run it through one full cycle of the task this week, note what felt clunky, and fix that one piece of friction. Do not add a second task until this one runs on autopilot.
