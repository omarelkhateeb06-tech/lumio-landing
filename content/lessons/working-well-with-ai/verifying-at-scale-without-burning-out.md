## reading

There is a quiet trap in using AI a lot: verification fatigue. At first you check everything carefully. Then it gets tiring, so you check less, then you check nothing, and one day an invented figure ends up in front of your boss. The problem was never your diligence. It was trying to check everything at the same intensity, which nobody can sustain.

The fix is to stop treating verification as one big chore and turn it into tiers, so your effort lands where the risk actually is.

**Tier 1, the glance.** For low-stakes, easily-judged output, such as a reworded email or a brainstorm, one read for sense and tone is enough. No sourcing, no cross-checking. Most daily AI use lives here, and trying to forensically verify it is the main cause of burnout.

**Tier 2, the targeted check.** For output that matters but is not dangerous, such as a report summary or a draft going to a client, do not re-verify everything. Pull out only the specific claims (numbers, names, dates) and check those against the source. The structure and wording you can trust.

**Tier 3, the full verify.** For high-stakes output, such as anything legal, medical, financial, or public, every factual claim gets confirmed against a primary source, and a second person reviews where possible. This is slow on purpose, and you only pay that cost for the small slice of work that warrants it.

The skill is sorting incoming AI work into the right tier in two seconds, so you spend your limited checking energy on the 10 percent that can actually hurt you. Done consistently, this is what lets you use AI heavily and still sleep at night.

## mini_project

Build your own three-tier verification routine and write it down so it becomes a habit instead of a daily decision. First, list the five most common types of AI output you produce in a typical week (for example: internal emails, meeting summaries, client-facing drafts, data lookups, social posts). Second, assign each one to a tier: glance, targeted check, or full verify, and write one sentence on what "done checking" means for that tier. Third, for your Tier 2 work, write a reusable instruction you can paste into the AI to make its claims easy to check, such as asking it to list every number and name separately. Keep the finished routine somewhere visible for two weeks and adjust any item that turns out to be in the wrong tier. The goal is a system you actually follow, not a perfect one you abandon.
