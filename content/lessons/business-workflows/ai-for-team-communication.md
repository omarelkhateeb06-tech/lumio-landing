## reading

Your team sends the same kinds of messages all the time: status updates, project kickoffs, deadline reminders, meeting recaps, bad-news notices. Each one gets written from scratch, slightly differently, by whoever happens to be doing it. That is wasted effort and inconsistent results. A small library of AI-built templates fixes both.

The idea is to use AI once to create a strong, reusable template for each common message, then fill in the specifics each time instead of starting over.

Here is how to build a good template with AI. Describe the message type, the audience, and what it always needs to include. "Create a reusable template for a weekly project status update to stakeholders. It should always cover progress, risks, and next steps, in a calm and confident tone. Mark the parts I fill in each week with brackets."

What comes back is a structure you can use every week: clear sections, the right tone baked in, and obvious blanks for the specifics. You are no longer writing the message, you are just dropping in this week's details.

The communication moments most worth templating:

**Status updates.** Same shape every time, so a template saves real effort.

**Bad news or delays.** A good template helps you stay calm, clear, and accountable when it is tempting to ramble or get defensive.

**Recaps and follow-ups.** A template ensures action items and owners never get dropped.

**Onboarding and how-to messages.** Write the explanation well once, reuse it forever.

The payoff is twofold: less time spent on routine writing, and more consistent, professional communication across the whole team. Build the templates once, and the quality holds even on a busy day.

## mini_project

Identify the three messages your team sends most often. Common picks: a weekly status update, a meeting recap with action items, and a delay or bad-news notice. For each one, ask AI to build a reusable template: describe the audience, the tone, and what the message must always include, and ask it to mark the fill-in parts with brackets. Review each template, adjust the wording to fit how your team actually talks, and save all three somewhere your team can find them. You now have a starter communication library that turns routine writing into quick fill-in-the-blank work.
