## reading

Differentiation is one of those words that sounds reasonable in a staff meeting and feels impossible at 9pm. The principle is sound. A single reading or task rarely fits every student in the room. But building a striving-reader version, a grade-level version, and an extension for the kids who race ahead has always meant doing the work two or three times over. So most of us pick one version and hope the rest of the class catches up or coasts.

AI changes the math, because differentiation is mostly transformation, and transformation is exactly what these models are good at. You write or choose one strong base text. Then you ask the model to produce the variations. You are not asking it to invent content. You are asking it to recast content you already trust into shapes that fit different readers. That is a much smaller and much safer job, and it is one a model can do well.

Everything starts from quality. The base text has to be accurate and well written, because every version inherits whatever you feed it. A vague or sloppy base text produces vague and sloppy levels. A precise one produces precise levels. So the first move is always the same. Get one strong text right, then transform it. Once you have that, you name your learners concretely. "Rewrite this passage at a 3rd grade reading level for students still building vocabulary, and keep every key fact" gives you a real support. "Create three comprehension questions that climb in difficulty, and make the last one open-ended" gives you a ladder instead of a worksheet. "Write a two-sentence challenge prompt for students who finish early that deepens thinking rather than just adding more reading" gives you the extension you never had time to build.

## reading

Here is what that looks like in a real room. Take a 5th grade science class working through a paragraph on the water cycle. You have a few striving readers who lose the thread when sentences run long, and two students who finish everything in four minutes and start drawing on their desks. You feed the AI your one solid paragraph. You ask for a simplified version at roughly a 3rd grade level that keeps all four stages, evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection, named and in order. You ask for the grade-level version lightly cleaned up. Then you ask for an extension: "A puddle disappears on a sunny day but there was no rain. Which stage explains where the water went, and what would have to happen for it to come back as rain?" Now your striving readers get the same science in sentences they can hold, and your early finishers get a question that makes them reason instead of just read more.

Take a different case. A middle-school social studies class is reading a textbook passage on the causes of a historical conflict, and you have a newcomer English-language learner who reads fluently in her first language but is still building academic English. You do not want to dumb the content down, because she is perfectly capable of the thinking. You want the language load lowered while the ideas stay intact. So you ask the AI to rewrite the passage with shorter sentences, plainer connective words, and a short glossary of the five hardest academic terms with one-line definitions, while keeping every cause and every name. She gets access to the same argument her classmates are wrestling with, just through a door she can actually walk through. The history does not change. The sentence length and the vocabulary scaffolding do.

## reading

Take a third. A 4th grade class is reading a short excerpt from a primary source, a letter or a speech in older, formal English, and you have a struggling reader who shuts down the moment the language feels old. You ask the AI for a simplified retelling that keeps the speaker's main point and one memorable line of the original quoted directly, so the student still touches the real voice but is not drowning in the syntax. You pair it with the original side by side. The struggling reader gets a way in, and the authentic source stays in the room rather than getting cut for being too hard.

In all three cases the pattern is identical. One base text in, several right-sized versions out, and the key facts preserved across every one. The model handles the recasting. You handle the judgment about whether each version actually serves the child you had in mind.

The result is not a robot teaching your class. It is you, finally able to hand three students three right-sized versions of the same lesson, because making them took fifteen minutes instead of three hours. Differentiation stops being the thing you skip when you are tired and becomes the thing you edit when you have the materials in front of you. That is a real shift. You move from building from scratch to reviewing a strong draft, and reviewing is faster, calmer, and far more sustainable across a full week of planning.

## reading

A few predictable mistakes turn this from a time-saver into a liability. They are worth knowing before you lean on the tool in front of real students.

The first is trusting a simplified version without reading it against the original. Simplifying is exactly the moment meaning slips. The model shortens a sentence and quietly drops a qualifier, and now your 3rd grade water cycle text says rain comes from clouds without mentioning that the water evaporated first, which breaks the whole cycle. Or a history passage gets simplified and a cause becomes the effect, or "one of several reasons" becomes "the reason." In science and history especially, a small flattening can produce something that is not just easier but wrong, and your striving readers are the least equipped to catch it. So you always read the simplified version against the source and confirm every key fact survived the trip. This check takes two minutes and it is non-negotiable.

The second is pasting a student's private information into a public tool. It is tempting to give the AI full context: "Adapt this for Marcus, who has an IEP for dyslexia and a reading goal of, " and then the specifics. Stop there. A student's name, IEP details, diagnoses, or any identifying information should not go into a public AI tool, because you do not control where that text lives afterward. You can get an excellent accommodation without naming the child. Describe the need, not the student. "Lower the language load and chunk the text for a reader who decodes slowly" gets you the support. "for Marcus, IEP for dyslexia" gets you a privacy problem you did not need.

## reading

The third is treating the AI's accommodation as authority. The model can draft a smart-sounding support, and it will sound confident doing it. But it has never met your student. Only you know that this particular child shuts down at long paragraphs but lights up with a question, or that this newcomer is academically advanced and would be insulted by a baby-talk version. The AI drafts the accommodation. You decide whether it actually serves the kid. When the model's suggestion and your read of the child disagree, you win, every time. The tool is a fast first drafter, not the person who knows the student.

The thread tying all three together is the same. AI is fast and fluent, and fluent is easy to trust. Your job is to stay the editor. Check the facts, protect the privacy, and keep the final call about each child where it belongs, with you.

## mini_project

Pick one text or task you are teaching this week and one real range of learners in your class, for example a science paragraph for a group with a few striving readers and two students who finish early. Feed the AI your single base text and ask for three versions: a lower reading-level version that keeps all the facts, a set of comprehension questions that climb in difficulty, and a short extension prompt for early finishers. Then do the part only you can do. Read the simplified version against the original and confirm nothing important got lost, and check that each version fits the specific students you had in mind.

- Choose one accurate base text and write one sentence naming your actual learners and their needs, without using any student's name or private details.
- Prompt the AI for the three versions, requiring that every key fact stays intact and that it flags any spot where simplifying might change the meaning.
- Read the simplified version line by line against the original, fix anything that drifted, and decide for each version whether it truly serves the child it is for.
