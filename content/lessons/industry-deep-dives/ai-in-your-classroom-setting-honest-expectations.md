## reading

The conversation about AI in schools often skips the most useful step. We debate whether students should use it while the students themselves are already using it, with no shared understanding of what is fair and what is cheating. Silence is not a neutral policy. It just means every student is guessing, and the cautious ones lose out to the bold ones.

Setting honest expectations means being clear about a real distinction: AI is powerful where it supports the thinking, and harmful where it replaces the thinking your assignment exists to build. Using it to brainstorm angles, get unstuck, or check grammar can deepen the work. Using it to generate the essay whose entire purpose was to make the student wrestle with the argument hollows out the learning. The line is not the tool. It is what skill the task was meant to grow.

You can use AI itself to draft this policy, and it helps to. Ask it to write a short, plain-language classroom AI policy for your grade level that distinguishes allowed support from off-limits replacement, with concrete examples for each. But the judgment about where the line falls is yours, because only you know what each assignment is teaching. A draft that bans AI entirely or allows it everywhere has missed the point you have to supply.

Two things make a policy actually work. State it in language a student and a parent can understand, not policy jargon, and give examples, because "use AI responsibly" means nothing without "yes to this, no to that." And model the honesty you are asking for, including showing students how you use AI in your own work and where you stop. Expectations you name plainly, and live yourself, are the ones students can follow.

## mini_project

Draft a real AI policy for one class you teach. Start by deciding the line yourself for one upcoming assignment: what is the specific skill it is meant to build, and therefore what AI help would support that versus replace it. Then ask the AI to write a short, plain-language policy with concrete yes and no examples at your grade level. Edit it so the line matches your judgment, not a generic default, and so it sounds like something you would actually say. As a final step, write one honest sentence you could share with students about how you use AI in your own work and where you stop, because the expectations you model are the ones they follow.
