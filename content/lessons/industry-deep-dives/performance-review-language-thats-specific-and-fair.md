## reading

Performance reviews fail in a predictable way, and once you see the pattern you cannot unsee it. Under time pressure, a manager sits down with twelve reviews due Friday and reaches for the phrases that come fastest: "strong communicator," "needs to be more proactive," "exceeds expectations," "great team player." These feel safe and say almost nothing. The employee reads "needs to be more proactive" and has no idea what to change on Monday morning. Be proactive about what? In which meeting? Compared to whom? The phrase points at a vague impression, not an action.

Vague language causes three specific problems, and they stack. The first is that the employee cannot act on it. Feedback that does not name a behavior cannot be repeated or corrected, so the person stays exactly where they were, and next quarter you write the same recycled line again. The second is that it does not hold up if a decision is ever questioned. If that review feeds a promotion you gave or a raise you withheld, and someone asks you to explain the basis, "needs to be more proactive" is not a basis. It is a feeling wearing a tie. The third, and the most serious, is that vague language is where bias hides. Unexamined impressions, who reminds you of yourself, who you find easy to like, slide in under cover of phrases like "not quite leadership material" or "a bit rough around the edges." Specific, behavior-based language forces those impressions into the open, where you can check whether they are fair. That is precisely why the lazy phrase feels more comfortable: it never makes you prove your case.

## reading

So the fix is known. Tie every statement to an observed behavior and its impact. "She led the Q3 campaign launch and hit the deadline despite a vendor delay by reworking the timeline herself" is something the employee can recognize, repeat, and be proud of. It is also something you can defend to anyone who asks. The catch is that writing this way is slow. Naming the example, recalling the impact, phrasing it cleanly and constructively, across a whole team, takes real time you do not have during review season. So the specific version gets skipped, and the vague version wins by default. This is not a character flaw in managers. It is a time problem.

This is exactly where AI earns its place, and it comes with one firm condition that you should write on a sticky note: it works FROM your observations, never IN PLACE OF them. You supply the real examples and your honest judgment. The model turns your rough, messy notes into clear, specific, professionally worded feedback that names the example and its impact. Picture Dana, a team lead writing a quarterly review for Priya, a marketing coordinator, at 9 p.m. with the deadline tomorrow. Dana types her real notes the way she would mutter them to herself: "led Q3 launch, hit deadline even though the vendor was late, reworked the timeline alone. Solid on execution. Growth area: waits to be asked before flagging concerns, so the budget risk surfaced late." Then she gives the model a clear instruction. "Turn each of these points into specific, behavior-based feedback that names the example and its impact, written constructively. Keep my assessment intact. Do not soften it, do not inflate it, and do not add any example I did not give you."

## reading

What comes back reads like the review Dana wanted to write and did not have ninety minutes to phrase. The execution note becomes a clear paragraph that credits the timeline rework and the on-time delivery. The growth area becomes a constructive forward action: "When you spot a budget or timeline risk, raise it early rather than waiting to be asked, so the team can adjust while there is still room to act." The substance, the rating, the examples, the judgment, every bit of it is Dana's. The model only did the wording. That is the whole trick, and it is a real time saver, because phrasing is the slow part and judgment is the fast part for someone who actually watched the work happen.

Notice what this does for an HRBP coaching managers, too. When Marcus, an HR business partner, sits with a manager who keeps writing "she's a strong communicator," he is not really fighting the manager's opinion. He is fighting the blank page and the clock. Pointing the manager toward this workflow, dump your honest notes, let the model phrase them, then check the result, gives the manager a way to be specific without spending the night on it. The coaching shifts from "be more specific, somehow" to "here is a repeatable way to be specific in the time you have." That is a far easier ask, and it is one managers actually adopt because it removes the cost that made them vague in the first place.

The judgment stays human because the consequences are human. A review can move a salary, decide a promotion, or sit in a file that someone reads a year from now. The words have to be both fair and true, and no tool can carry that weight for you. AI gives you back the time the careful wording used to cost. What you do with that time, looking hard at whether your feedback is fair, is the part that is yours and only yours.

## reading

The workflow is simple, so most of the ways it goes wrong come from quietly handing the AI a job that was never supposed to leave your hands. Watch for these.

The first and worst mistake is letting the model generate the assessment itself. A tired team lead types "write a strong performance review for a solid marketing coordinator" and pastes the result straight in. It reads smoothly and it is worthless, because it is built from a generic idea of "solid coordinator," not from anything Priya actually did. It will praise strengths she may not have and miss the timeline rework that was her best work all quarter. Worse, generic boilerplate is exactly the kind of detached, one-size-fits-all language that treats people unfairly, because it never looks at the person. The rule holds with no exceptions: if the AI is inventing the evaluation, you have already lost. It must work from your specifics.

The second mistake is skipping the fairness and accuracy pass because the draft looks polished. Polish is seductive. A clean, confident paragraph feels finished, so you stop reading critically. But the model can quietly drift: it may add an example you never gave it ("consistently mentored junior staff" when you said nothing of the kind), or inflate "hit the deadline" into "delivered flawless, industry-leading results." Both are now false statements in a document with career consequences. Read every sentence and ask: did I actually observe this? If you did not give it to the model, it does not belong in the review.

## reading

The third mistake is letting the language slide from behavior toward personality. "Raised the budget risk late" is a behavior Priya can change. "Is not a natural leader" is a verdict on who she is, and it is neither fair nor useful. Models will sometimes reach for personality framing because it sounds evaluative. Catch it and pull it back to what the person did and what resulted.

The fourth mistake is inconsistent tone across the team. Because you write each review separately, sometimes on different days and in different moods, the model can phrase the same level of performance more warmly for one person and more coolly for another. Marcus, the HRBP, sees this constantly: two coordinators who performed the same get reviews that read very differently, and the gap usually tracks who the manager likes. After drafting, line your reviews up side by side and check that comparable performance gets comparable language. The fix is reading them as a set, not one at a time.

The last mistake is treating the polished draft as the finish line. It is the starting line. The model gives you a strong first phrasing; your judgment, your fairness check, and your edits are what make it ready to sign. The tool saved you the slow part. It did not take over the part that matters.

## mini_project

Take a real review you need to write, or practice on one you wrote recently. Start by jotting your honest, rough notes about the person the way you would say them out loud: specific things they did, each with the example and its impact, including at least one genuine growth area. Hand the AI your notes and ask it to turn each point into specific, behavior-based language without inflating, softening, or inventing anything, framing any growth area as a constructive forward action. Then run the fairness pass before you trust a word of it, and notice how much sharper and more useful the result is than the vague version you would have written under deadline.

- Confirm every statement traces to something you actually observed, and cut any example you did not supply.
- Check that the feedback targets behavior and results rather than personality, rewriting any verdict-on-the-person back into a describable action.
- Compare the tone against how you would phrase the same performance for a different team member, and even out any gap so comparable work gets comparable language.
