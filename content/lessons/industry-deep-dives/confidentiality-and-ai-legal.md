## reading

For legal professionals, confidentiality is not a preference, it is a professional duty. It is one of the first things you learned and one of the few rules with no soft edges. AI tools create a real risk to that duty if you are not careful, because what you type into a public tool can leave your control the instant you hit enter. The good news is that you can still use AI for real legal work every day. You just need a few habits that keep privileged material on the right side of the line. This lesson is that set of habits.

Start with how these tools actually handle your text, because the risk is invisible if you do not understand it. When you type into a free, public AI service, your input does not just vanish after it answers. It may be stored on the company's servers, reviewed by its staff for quality and safety, and used to train future versions of the model. That last part matters most. Information used in training can resurface later in ways no one can fully predict or pull back. None of that is compatible with privileged and confidential client information. Pasting a client's name, their matter details, or a privileged communication into a public tool can be a breach even if nothing visibly goes wrong, because the disclosure already happened the moment the text was sent.

Picture a concrete example. A paralegal at a small firm is helping with an employment dispute. The client, a warehouse worker, says she was fired days after reporting blocked fire exits to her manager. To save time, the paralegal pastes the full story into a free chatbot, client name and employer included, and asks for a list of legal issues. The summary that comes back is genuinely useful. But the client's identity, the employer's name, and the heart of a privileged matter have now left the firm and entered an outside company's system. Nothing on screen looks wrong. The breach is real anyway. Now take a second example from litigation. An assistant pastes a draft settlement figure and the opposing party's name into a public tool to clean up the language. That figure and that pairing are exactly what the firm is obligated to keep private, and they are now sitting in a system the firm does not control. In both cases the work felt routine, and that is precisely why the risk is easy to miss.

So here are the rules that keep you safe. They are simple, and together they cover almost every situation you will face.

**Treat public AI tools as untrusted with confidential data.** The mental model that works: a free public tool is like talking in a crowded room. You would not discuss a client's matter at full volume in a coffee shop, so do not type anything privileged or client-confidential into a public tool. If you would not say it out loud among strangers, do not paste it.

**Genericize before you paste.** You can still get excellent help by removing the identifying and sensitive parts first. Replace the client name with [client], the specific company with [a manufacturing company], the dollar figures and dates with placeholders. Take a contract matter as an example. Instead of pasting "review the indemnification clause in the supply agreement between Halloran Foods and Tri-State Logistics," you can ask the AI to "explain what to check in an indemnification clause in a supply agreement between [a food company] and [a logistics provider]." The AI drafts and summarizes just as well from the generic version, and nothing client-identifying ever leaves your firm.

**Use approved, private tools for sensitive work.** Many firms now provide an enterprise AI tool backed by a contract that prohibits training on your data and adds stronger privacy protections. For anything genuinely confidential, use that approved tool, not a personal free account. If you do not know what your firm permits, ask before you paste, not after. A two-minute question to your supervisor or IT is far cheaper than a disclosure you cannot reverse.

**When unsure, keep it out.** If you are not certain whether something is safe to share, that hesitation is your answer. Genericize it or keep it offline. The cost of being careful is a few extra minutes. The cost of being wrong is a breach of a client's trust and your professional duty.

**Know your obligations.** Confidentiality rules apply to you directly, and emerging AI-specific guidance from bar associations and courts increasingly speaks to exactly this situation. The convenience of a tool never overrides your duty to the client. You are responsible for what you put into a tool, the same way you are responsible for what you put in an email.

None of this means avoiding AI. It means being deliberate. Generic inputs go into public tools, sensitive work goes only into approved ones, and client confidentiality stays protected at every step. Once these habits are automatic, you get the speed of AI without ever putting a client at risk.

## reading

The rules are clear, so the trouble usually comes from small habits that feel harmless in the moment. Here are the mistakes that catch careful people, and why each one still counts as a breach.

**Pasting full matter details and the client name, then deleting the chat.** This is the most common one, and the deletion is what makes it feel safe. It is not. The disclosure happened the instant you sent the text. Deleting the conversation on your screen does nothing about the copy that already reached the company's servers. You cannot undo a disclosure by closing a window.

**Adding an instruction that tells the AI to "keep this confidential."** It feels responsible, like a disclaimer at the bottom of an email. But the AI does not enforce your prompt. How your input is stored, reviewed, and used is set by the company's data policy, not by a sentence you typed. Telling a public tool to keep a secret does not change what the tool is allowed to do with it.

**Including just the client name, thinking a name alone is harmless.** A name tied to a matter is confidential. If you ask a public tool a question about "the Delgado custody case," you have linked a real person to a legal proceeding, which is exactly the kind of fact you are obligated to protect. The name plus the context is the breach, even without the details.

**Using a personal free account for sensitive work instead of the firm's approved tool.** Maybe the approved tool is slower to open, or you are working from home, so you reach for the free one out of habit. The firm's tool exists precisely because it has a contract that protects client data. Skipping it for convenience puts the matter into a system with none of those protections.

**Assuming nothing went wrong because nothing visibly broke.** AI tools do not show an error when you overshare. You get a helpful answer, the day moves on, and the breach is silent. The absence of an alarm is not proof of safety. Judge your own actions by what you sent, not by whether anything looked wrong on screen.

The thread running through all five is the same. Confidentiality is breached at the moment of disclosure, not at the moment something visibly fails. None of these mistakes come from carelessness about the client. They come from trusting the wrong signals, a deleted window, a polite instruction, the lack of an error message, and assuming those signals mean you are safe. Build your habits around the moment of disclosure instead, and these mistakes stop happening.

## mini_project

Take one real, active matter from your current workload and produce a genericized version you could safely use with a public AI tool. The goal is a clean, reusable draft that carries the legal substance of the matter while stripping every detail that could identify the client. When you finish, you will have a template you can apply to the next matter in minutes.

- Write the matter out in one or two sentences exactly as you would describe it internally, with the real client name, the company, the figures, and the key dates included.
- Rewrite it replacing the client name with [client], the company with a generic descriptor like [a manufacturing company], and every figure, date, and identifying fact with a bracketed placeholder, then read it back and confirm that nothing left in it could identify the client or the matter.
- Mark which parts of the work you could safely run in a public tool using the generic version, and which parts must wait for your firm's approved private tool, so you know exactly where each step belongs.
