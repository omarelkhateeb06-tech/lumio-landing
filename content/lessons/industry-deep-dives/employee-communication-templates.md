## reading

Think about the messages that leave your outbox in a normal month. An onboarding welcome to a new hire. A benefits-enrollment reminder when the window opens. A policy-change announcement when the handbook updates. A return-to-office notice. Every so often, a sensitive announcement that you draft slowly and carefully. If you line these up, you notice something useful: most of them are repeatable in shape but personal in detail. The skeleton of a benefits reminder is the same every year. Only the dates, the plan year, the enrollment link, and the person's name change. That gap between what stays the same and what changes is exactly where AI earns its keep.

The problem with writing each message from scratch is not only that it is slow, though it is slow. The bigger cost is inconsistency. When you write every onboarding welcome a little differently, new hires get a slightly different first impression depending on who sent it and how rushed that day was. When two policy announcements describe the same rule in different words, employees notice, and sometimes the two versions quietly contradict each other. An inconsistent employee experience is not a small thing. It is the difference between a company that feels organized and one that feels like it is improvising. Templates fix both problems at once: they make routine communication fast, and they make it consistent.

The move that makes this work is to ask AI for reusable STRUCTURES, not one-off messages. There is a real difference. If you ask for "an email reminding people that benefits enrollment closes Friday," you get a single finished message about this Friday, useless next year. If you ask for "a reusable template for a benefits-enrollment-window reminder, with clear bracketed placeholders for everything that changes, a warm and plain tone, and short enough that people actually read it," you get something you can use every year for the rest of your career. A good template has three qualities. It has obvious blanks, written as bracketed placeholders like [employee name] and [enrollment deadline], so anyone can see at a glance what needs filling in. It has a tone you chose deliberately, because tone is a decision, not an accident. And it has a reusable structure you do not have to rebuild each time. Picture an HR generalist who sends benefits reminders every single year. Once she has a strong template, that yearly task drops from a blank-page chore to a five-minute fill-in-the-blanks job.

The discipline that keeps this safe lives entirely in the placeholders and the personalization. The template is the 80 percent that is the same on every send. The 20 percent that changes, the names, the dates, the specific situation, is yours to fill in, and you fill it in from your real records. This is the rule worth tattooing on the inside of your eyelids: you never paste employee data into the AI tool to generate the template. The structure is generic on purpose. It says [employee name], not Maria Santos. It says [start date], not March 3rd. Keeping it generic is what makes it safe to draft, store, and reuse, because nothing private ever touched the tool. A people-ops coordinator standardizing onboarding messages across the company should build one clean, generic welcome template and then personalize each send from the HRIS, never the other way around. The template lives in the tool's world, generic and reusable. The real details live in your systems, private and accurate. You bridge the two by hand at send time.

Now the part that takes judgment, because not every message belongs in a template library in the same way. Templates are perfect for routine, informational messages. They are only a STARTING POINT, not a script, for emotionally weighted ones. Layoffs, performance concerns, news that touches someone's personal circumstances: in these moments a templated tone reads as cold exactly when warmth matters most. Imagine an HRBP who is about to reuse a generic template for a layoff notice because it is faster. Faster, yes, and also a mistake, because the person reading it can feel boilerplate, and feeling like a form letter at the worst moment of their work life is its own small cruelty. For these messages, let the template give you structure and remind you what to cover, then personalize heavily so the human is unmistakable.

The second caution is quieter but just as important: review every template for accuracy and tone before it joins your library. A template is a force multiplier, and multipliers work in both directions. A polished, correct template repeated fifty times saves you enormous effort. A template with a wrong date format, an outdated policy reference, or one phrase that lands a little cold repeats that exact error fifty times before anyone catches it. Get the base right once, with real attention, because that attention pays off on every future send. Then personalize each message, and reserve your genuine human focus for the communications that truly need it. That is the whole craft: build the 80 percent carefully, fill the 20 percent from your own records, and know which messages deserve more than a template can give.

## reading

The idea of a template library is simple, so the ways it goes wrong are usually about discipline slipping, not about misunderstanding the concept. Here are the failures that show up most often, and how to avoid each one.

The first and most serious mistake is pasting real employee data into the tool to "make the template better." It feels harmless. You think, if I give it Maria's actual start date and team, it will write something more specific. But the moment you do that, you have handed private information to an outside tool for no good reason, and you have made a template that is secretly about one person instead of being a clean generic structure. The whole point is that the template says [employee name] and [start date]. Keep it generic in the tool, and fill the real details from your HRIS at send time. If you ever feel tempted to paste real data, that is your signal that you are personalizing in the wrong place.

The second mistake is shipping a template straight from the AI into your library without reading it as if you were the employee receiving it. AI drafts are confident and polished, which is exactly why an error hides well inside them. A wrong default-outcome description in a benefits reminder, an outdated policy name, a date written in a confusing format: any of these, repeated across fifty sends, becomes fifty small problems. The fix costs almost nothing. Read every template once, slowly, checking accuracy and tone, before it earns a place in the library. The HR generalist who reviews her benefits template carefully in year one saves herself five years of repeating the same buried error.

The third mistake is treating every message type as equally template-friendly. Routine and informational, like enrollment reminders and return-to-office notices, are ideal for send-as-is templates once personalized. Emotionally weighted messages are not. The HRBP who reuses a layoff template as a script, changing only the name, sends something that reads as a form letter at the moment a person most needs to feel seen. The lesson is not "never template sensitive messages." It is "mark them clearly as personalize-heavily starting points," so the structure helps you without flattening the humanity out of the message.

The last mistake is subtler: building too many slightly different templates for the same situation. If three coordinators each generate their own onboarding welcome, you are back to the inconsistency you were trying to escape. Pick one strong version per message type, agree on it, and reuse it. One good template beats five competing ones. Consistency is the prize, and it only shows up when the library has a single clear answer for each routine message you send.

## mini_project

Build the first shelf of your own template library. List the five or six messages you send most often, ranging from routine reminders to the occasional sensitive announcement, then use AI to draft reusable structures for the routine ones and mark the sensitive ones clearly. Keep every template generic, with bracketed placeholders and no real employee data anywhere in the tool, and read each one slowly for accuracy and tone before it earns a place in your library.

- Draft three reusable templates for routine messages (for example a benefits reminder, an onboarding welcome, and a return-to-office notice), each with clear bracketed placeholders and a tone you chose on purpose.
- Review each template as if you were the employee receiving it, fixing any wrong date format, outdated reference, or phrase that lands cold, because an error in the template repeats on every future send.
- Identify one emotionally weighted message on your list and label it a personalize-heavily starting point, not a send-as-is script, so future-you remembers to add real warmth and human attention.
