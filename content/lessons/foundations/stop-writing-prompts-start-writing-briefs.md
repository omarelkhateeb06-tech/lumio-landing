## reading

A prompt is a question. A brief is a document. The distinction matters because ChatGPT isn't a search engine: it's a collaborator. And you wouldn't hand a collaborator a single sentence and expect professional output.

**What a brief includes that a prompt doesn't:**

**Context:** What's the situation? Who's the audience? What's already been decided? Adding "We're launching a B2B analytics feature to a list of 3,000 SMB customers who have been on our waitlist for 6 months" transforms a generic prompt into something the model can actually work with.

**Stakes:** Why does this matter? What does success look like? When the model understands what you're trying to accomplish, it makes better tradeoffs.

**Examples:** If you have a voice or a previous output you liked, paste it in. "Write in a tone similar to this: [paste]" is the fastest path to matching your style.

**Rejection criteria:** What would make the output bad? "Don't use corporate jargon. Don't start with 'We're excited to announce.' Don't exceed 150 words." These constraints are as important as the instructions.

The transition from prompting to briefing is the single biggest skill shift in working with AI. It takes 60 more seconds and saves 20 minutes of revision.

## mini_project

Your 5-minute exercise: Write a brief (not a prompt) for the next work task on your to-do list. Include context, stakes, any style examples, and at least two rejection criteria. Run it and notice what's different.
