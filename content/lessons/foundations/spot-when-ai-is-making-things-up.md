## reading

Hallucination isn't a bug they're going to fix next month. It's a structural feature of how language models work. They predict the most plausible-sounding next token, which means they'll invent a plausible-sounding answer even when the right answer is "I don't know."

**The four hallucination tells:**

**1. Suspiciously specific details.** "The study, published in the Journal of Applied Psychology in 2019, found that 73% of..." Invented citations are often precise in the wrong way. Always verify any citation before using it.

**2. Confident claims about recent events.** Anything after the model's training cutoff is either fabricated or outdated. If it's telling you about something recent without hedging, it's guessing.

**3. No hedging on genuinely uncertain territory.** Good models hedge: "I'm not certain, but..." Bad answers charge straight through uncertainty.

**4. Internal contradictions.** Hallucinations often contradict themselves within the same response. Read the full answer before acting on it.

The fix isn't to stop using AI: it's to use it for the right things. Use it for synthesis and generation where "mostly right" is fine. Verify anything you'd stake your reputation on.

## mini_project

Your 5-minute exercise: Ask ChatGPT to 'list three academic studies on remote work productivity.' Then try to verify one: find the journal, the year, the authors. What did you find?
