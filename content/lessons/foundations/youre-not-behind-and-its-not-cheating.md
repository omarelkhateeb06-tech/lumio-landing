## reading

If you've been avoiding AI, it's usually one of two reasons. Either you feel like everyone else already figured it out and you missed the bus, or some part of you feels that leaning on it is cutting corners. Let's take both apart, because both are holding you back for no good reason.

**You're not behind.** The tools most people use today became genuinely useful only recently. The person at work who seems fluent has been at it for months, not decades. There is no advanced track you failed to enroll in. Almost everyone is figuring this out at the same time, in public, by trial and error. A few focused hours puts you ahead of most of your colleagues, not because you're special, but because most people haven't sat down to actually try.

**It's not cheating.** When calculators arrived, people worried that using them was a way to avoid real math. Today nobody thinks twice. AI is the same kind of shift. Using it to draft an email, summarize a report, or untangle a messy idea is not skipping the work: it's doing the work faster so your judgment goes where it matters. The skill that counts now is knowing what to ask, spotting when the answer is wrong, and deciding what to keep. That is real work, and it's the skill this course builds.

**The only real mistake is not starting.** Every hour you wait, the gap between you and the people who started feels bigger, even though it isn't. The fix is not to study harder. It's to open the tool and use it for one small thing today. Competence comes from reps, not from reading about reps.

So drop the guilt and drop the catching-up story. You're early, you're allowed, and the next lesson gets your hands dirty.
