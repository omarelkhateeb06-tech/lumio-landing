## reading

Most of this course has been about using AI well. This lesson is about the other half of judgment: deciding where you will not use it, and noticing when its answers are quietly skewed. The strongest AI users are not the ones who automate everything. They are the ones who chose their limits deliberately, instead of drifting into using the tool everywhere by default.

**Set your own lines, before you are in the moment.** There are places where, on reflection, you may not want AI: the genuinely personal message that should cost you something to write, the decision you believe you should sit with yourself, the skill you are deliberately keeping sharp, the situation where speed is not actually the point. These are personal calls, not rules anyone can hand you. But deciding them in advance means you act from values rather than convenience when the shortcut is tempting.

**Know that AI carries bias.** AI learned from an enormous pile of human text, and it absorbed the patterns in it, including the skews. It can lean toward majority viewpoints, reflect stereotypes about who does what job, default to a Western or English-speaking frame, and present a contested opinion as settled fact. None of this is malicious; it is a mirror of its training data. But if you take its output as neutral truth, you inherit those biases without knowing it.

**Watch for the tells.** Be alert when AI presents one perspective as the obvious answer, makes assumptions about people based on roles or names, flattens a complex debate into a tidy consensus, or describes "normal" in a way that quietly excludes most of the world. Asking "whose viewpoint is this, and who is missing" surfaces what the smooth answer left out.

**Counter it actively.** When a balanced view matters, ask for it: "What are the strongest opposing perspectives? Whose experience might contradict this? What assumptions are you making about the people involved?" You can steer around bias once you remember to look for it.

The throughline of responsible AI use is staying in charge: of where the tool belongs in your life, and of whether you accept its framing or question it. Use it with intention, hold onto the parts that should stay human, and never mistake a fluent answer for an unbiased one.
