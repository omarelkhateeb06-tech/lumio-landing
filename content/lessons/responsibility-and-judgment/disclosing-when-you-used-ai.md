## reading

A genuinely confusing question in the AI age: when do you have to tell people you used it? Disclose everything and you sound absurd, nobody announces they used a calculator. Disclose nothing and you risk a real breach of trust, like a personal letter that turns out to be machine-written. There is no universal rule, but there is a reliable way to reason about it.

The guiding question is: **would this person feel misled if they found out?** Their reasonable expectation, not a fixed policy, is what determines whether disclosure matters.

**Routine assistance: no disclosure needed.** Using AI to draft a standard work email, clean up grammar, summarize a document for yourself, or brainstorm privately is like using any other tool. Nobody expects those to be done by hand, so there is nothing to disclose.

**Authorship and effort matter: disclose, or do not delegate.** When the value is partly in the fact that a human did it, a heartfelt thank-you, a personal recommendation, a creative work credited to you, condolences, the AI's involvement changes what the thing means. Here, either keep it genuinely yours or be transparent. Quietly passing off machine sentiment as your own is the case that burns trust.

**Stakes, accountability, or rules: disclose.** In academic work, regulated fields, journalism, legal filings, or anywhere a policy or professional standard applies, disclosure is often required, and the safe default is transparency. When being wrong has consequences, people deserve to know a tool was involved.

**When unsure, lean toward honesty, lightly.** You do not need a formal disclaimer. "I drafted this with some AI help and reviewed it" is often enough, and it costs you nothing while protecting the relationship.

The underlying principle is simple respect: people are entitled to an accurate sense of how something was made when that would matter to them. Get that right and disclosure stops being awkward. It becomes just another way of being trustworthy.
