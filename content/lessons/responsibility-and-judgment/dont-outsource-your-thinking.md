## reading

Here is a cost of AI that does not show up immediately. If you let it do all your thinking, your own ability to think gets weaker, the same way a muscle you stop using atrophies. People who outsource every draft slowly lose their writing voice. People who never plan a project themselves lose the instinct for it. The tool that made you faster this month can make you worse over a year, if you let it replace the thinking instead of sharpening it.

This is not an argument against AI. It is an argument for using it deliberately, so you keep the capabilities that make you valuable.

**Form your own take first.** Before you ask AI, spend two minutes on your own answer, even a rough one. Then bring AI in to challenge, extend, or polish it. This one habit keeps you as the thinker and the AI as the assistant, instead of the reverse. The thinking stays yours.

**Protect the skills you cannot afford to lose.** Decide which abilities are core to who you are professionally, the ones you would not want to rust, and keep doing those by hand often enough to stay sharp. Use AI freely on everything else. A writer should still write sometimes; an analyst should still reason through numbers sometimes.

**Use it to learn, not just to answer.** When AI solves something, do not just take the result. Ask it to explain why, so you come away understanding more than you did. Used this way, AI builds your judgment instead of substituting for it.

**Notice the dependency creep.** If you find you can no longer start a task without AI, or you cannot judge whether its output is any good, that is the warning sign. The fix is to do a few unassisted reps to rebuild the underlying skill.

The goal is a partnership where you stay the more capable one over time. AI should leave you sharper, with more range and better judgment, not hollowed out and unable to work without it. Which outcome you get is a choice you make every time you reach for it.
