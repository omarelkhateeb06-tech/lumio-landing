## reading

It is tempting, when a hard decision lands on your desk, to ask AI what to do and follow its answer. That feels efficient, and it is a quiet abdication. AI can be an excellent advisor: fast, tireless, able to lay out options and trade-offs you would have missed. But it is a poor decision-maker, because it does not carry your context, your values, or the consequences. The decision, and the accountability for it, has to stay with you.

The useful frame is to give AI the analyst's job, not the executive's. Let it do the work that informs a decision, and keep the deciding for yourself.

**Have it map the option space.** "Lay out the realistic options here, with the main pros and cons of each." This is where AI shines, expanding your view beyond the two choices you were stuck between.

**Make it surface trade-offs and risks.** "What am I trading off with each option? What could go wrong that I'm not seeing?" A good decision is rarely about the obvious upside; it is about the trade-offs, and AI is good at naming them.

**Stress-test your leaning.** Once you have a tentative call, have AI argue against it. "I'm leaning toward option B. Make the strongest case that I'm wrong." If your choice survives the attack, you decide with more confidence. If it does not, you just dodged a mistake.

**Then decide, with your context.** Here is the part AI cannot do: weigh the options against what you know that it does not. The relationship history, the team's morale, the thing your gut is telling you, the values you will not compromise. You fold that in, and you make the call.

The line to hold: AI helps you decide better; it does not decide for you. When the outcome lands, "the AI told me to" is not a defense anyone accepts, nor should it be. You used a good tool to think more clearly, and then you chose. That is what owning a decision looks like.

## mini_project

Take a real decision you are currently weighing and run it through an advisor-not-decider process, keeping the call firmly yours. First, write down your own initial leaning and the one or two things you are unsure about, before consulting AI at all. Second, use AI as an analyst: ask it to lay out the realistic options with pros and cons, then ask it to name the trade-offs and risks you might be missing, and finally ask it to make the strongest case against your current leaning. Third, set the AI output aside and write the decision yourself in a few sentences, explicitly noting the context only you have, the relationships, values, or constraints that AI never knew, and how they shaped your final call. The deliverable is your decision, on the record, informed by AI but owned by you. Notice how much clearer the reasoning is than if you had just asked it what to do.
