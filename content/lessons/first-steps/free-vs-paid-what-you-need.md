## reading

The moment you sign up, you'll see the pitch: a paid plan, usually around twenty dollars a month, promising the "smartest" model and more of everything. It's tempting to assume the real value is behind the paywall. For a beginner, it usually isn't. Here's how to think about it without overspending or under-equipping yourself.

**The free tier is a real product, not a crippled demo.** On the major assistants, the free version can draft, summarize, explain, brainstorm, and handle the everyday tasks this whole module is about. You can build the entire habit, and finish this course, without paying a cent. Start there. Spending money before you know what you need is just a more expensive way to procrastinate.

**Paid tiers buy three things, mostly.** When you do hit the limits, here's what your money actually gets you:
- **Smarter models for hard tasks.** The top-tier model reasons better on complex, multi-step problems. For rewriting an email, you won't notice. For untangling a thorny analysis, you might.
- **Higher limits.** Free tiers cap how much you can use the best model per day. Heavy users hit that wall; occasional users rarely do.
- **Extra features.** Things like file uploads, image generation, web browsing, or connecting to your documents are sometimes gated to paid plans, and this varies by tool.

**Upgrade when you can name the wall.** Don't pay on a vague feeling that paid is "better." Pay when you can finish this sentence: "I keep hitting *this specific limit* and it's slowing down *this real task*." Maybe you keep running out of the good model mid-afternoon, or you need to upload documents and can't. That's a real reason. "It might be better" is not.

**If your employer provides a tool, use that first.** Many workplaces hand out a paid Copilot or Gemini seat that's already connected to your email and files. That's a paid tier someone else is funding. Check before you buy your own.

Free until it pinches, then upgrade with a reason. That's the whole decision.
