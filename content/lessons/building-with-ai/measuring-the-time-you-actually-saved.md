## reading

"AI saves me so much time." Almost everyone who uses it says this, and almost no one can back it up with a number. That is a problem when your manager asks whether the subscription is worth it, when you want to justify rolling a tool out to your team, or when you are simply trying to decide where AI actually helps versus where it just feels productive. The fix is not complicated: measure it. A rough but honest before-and-after beats a confident guess every time.

The trap to avoid is counting only the fast part. AI writes the first draft in twenty seconds, and it is tempting to call that the whole saving. But then you spend ten minutes editing it, checking a fact it got wrong, and reshaping the tone. The real number is the total time of the new way, editing and verifying included, against the total time of the old way. Measure the honest version or you will overstate the gain and get caught.

**Pick a few recurring tasks worth measuring.** Do not try to measure everything. Choose three or four things you do often: the weekly status report, customer replies, summarizing a long document, drafting a routine email. Frequent tasks are where real time adds up, and they are easy to compare because you do them the same way each time.

**Time the old way first, honestly.** Before you change anything, note how long the task normally takes you start to finish. A stopwatch on your phone is enough. Do this a couple of times so you have a fair baseline rather than one unusually good or bad run.

**Time the new way, all of it.** Now do the same task with AI and time the entire thing: writing the prompt, waiting, reading the output, editing it, checking anything that needs checking, and finishing. The net saving is old time minus new time. Sometimes it is large. Sometimes, for a task AI is bad at, it is zero or negative, and that is genuinely useful to know.

**Track it over a couple of weeks, then total it up.** One measurement is an anecdote. A handful across two weeks is evidence. Add up the minutes saved per week, and you have a real figure: "AI saves me about four hours a week, mostly on reporting and first drafts." That sentence, backed by numbers, is worth far more than "it helps a lot."

The point is not to turn your job into a time-and-motion study. It is to replace a vague feeling with something you can defend, so you invest your AI effort where it truly pays off and can make the case to anyone who asks.

## mini_project

Prove your real AI time savings with a two-week measurement, then write up the result. First, pick three or four recurring tasks you do often and currently use or could use AI for, such as a weekly report, routine emails, or document summaries. Second, for each task, time the old way honestly start to finish at least once to set a baseline, then time the AI way including prompt writing, editing, and fact-checking, and record the net minutes saved per instance. Third, over two weeks, keep a simple running tally of how many times you did each task and the minutes saved each time, and flag any task where AI saved little or nothing. Finally, total it into a single clear figure with a short breakdown, for example "about four hours a week saved, mostly on reporting and first drafts, with customer replies saving almost nothing." The deliverable is a one-paragraph, numbers-backed summary of your real weekly time saving that you could hand to a manager, plus a short note on which tasks are not worth using AI for.
