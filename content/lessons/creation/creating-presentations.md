## reading

AI can help you build a presentation far faster, but there is a trap. If you let it generate the whole thing on autopilot, you get a deck that is technically complete and completely forgettable: vague bullet points, generic stock phrases, no spine. The skill is using AI for the parts it does well while keeping the parts that make a deck actually work.

Use AI for structure first. A good presentation is a story, not a pile of slides. Tell the AI your goal and your audience, and ask for the arc. "I am pitching a budget increase to leadership. Give me a slide-by-slide structure that builds a persuasive case, with one clear message per slide." A strong skeleton is half the battle.

Then use it to sharpen each slide's message. The most common deck mistake is slides crammed with text. Ask: "Rewrite this slide so it makes one clear point in under ten words, with two supporting sub-points." That alone makes a deck look professional.

Where you must stay in charge: the specifics and the proof. AI does not have your real numbers, your customer story, or the one example that will make leadership lean in. Those come from you. The structure and wording can be AI-assisted. The substance cannot be faked.

The other reason decks look "AI-made" is generic language: phrases like "leverage synergies" and "unlock value" that say nothing. Tell the AI explicitly to use plain, concrete language and to avoid buzzwords. Better yet, give it your real example and ask it to build the point around that.

Structure and clarity from AI, substance and judgment from you. That combination produces a deck that moves fast to build and still feels like a real person made it.
