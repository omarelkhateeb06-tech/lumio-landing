## reading

Here is a quiet superpower for anyone who creates anything: you do not have to start from scratch for every channel. Write one good thing, then use AI to turn it into the five different shapes you need.

Think about how much you already produce that gets used once and forgotten. A detailed report. A long email. A set of meeting notes. A webinar transcript. Each of those is raw material that could become several useful pieces with almost no extra effort.

The move is simple. Start with one strong source piece, then ask AI to transform it for a specific place and audience. The ideas stay the same. The format and tone change.

Some transformations that work well:

**Long to short.** "Turn this 1,000-word article into a 5-bullet summary for a busy executive."

**Formal to casual.** "Rewrite this report's key finding as a friendly two-sentence message for our team channel."

**One audience to another.** "I wrote this for customers. Now adapt it for our internal sales team, focusing on what they need to know to sell it."

**One format to another.** "Turn these meeting notes into a clear follow-up email with action items."

The reason this works is that AI is genuinely good at preserving meaning while changing form. You did the hard part, which is having something worth saying. The reshaping is the easy part, and now it is automatic.

A reminder: each repurposed version still deserves a quick read. Different audiences need different things, and you know your audiences better than the AI does. It reshapes, you approve.

## mini_project

Take one thing you have already written this month: a report, a long email, a set of notes, anything with real content in it. In a single AI chat, repurpose it into three different formats for three different places. For example: a short summary for a busy executive, a casual update for your team channel, and a follow-up email with clear action items. Paste the source piece once, then ask for each transformation in turn, naming the audience and format each time. Review each version, adjust anything that does not fit the audience, and notice how one piece of work now serves three purposes.
