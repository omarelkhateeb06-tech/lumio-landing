## reading

The cruelest part of writing is the blank page. Editing something bad is easy. Creating something from nothing is hard. AI flips the problem: it gives you a draft to react to, so you skip straight to the part your brain is good at.

But there is a right way and a wrong way to do this. The wrong way is "write me a blog post about productivity." You will get bland, forgettable filler that sounds like everyone else. The right way is to feed the AI enough of your thinking that the draft is genuinely yours to refine.

Before you ask for a draft, give it four things:

**The point of view.** What is the one thing you actually want to say? "Most productivity advice fails because it ignores energy, not time." A real angle beats a neutral topic every time.

**The audience.** Who is reading, and what do they already believe? "Busy managers who have tried and abandoned a dozen systems."

**The key points.** The two or three things that must be in there, even as rough notes.

**The shape.** Length, format, and tone. "About 400 words, conversational, no clichés."

Now ask for the draft. What comes back will not be perfect, and that is fine. It is a starting block, not a finish line. Your job now is the satisfying part: cut what is weak, sharpen what is strong, add the specific example only you know, and make it sound like you.

The honest truth: a first draft from AI is usually a B-minus. But a B-minus you can edit beats an A-plus you never start. And the editing is where your taste and expertise turn it into something good.
