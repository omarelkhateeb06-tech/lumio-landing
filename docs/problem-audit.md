# Lumio Problem Audit — Canonical Reference

The validated problem taxonomy underlying the curriculum.
Problems 1-150 = original audit. Bucket 4 = web-validated additions.
Problem numbers are cited throughout the curriculum spec and lesson metadata.

## Bucket 1 — Problems people have but don't know AI can solve (1-50)

### Communication & Writing
1. Spending 20 minutes drafting one email
2. Not knowing how to say "no" professionally
3. Translating blunt thoughts into diplomatic phrasing
4. Rewriting the same type of message from scratch every time
5. Adjusting tone for different audiences (boss vs. peer vs. client)
6. Responding to an angry customer without escalating
7. Catching up on a long email thread
8. Writing a meeting recap people actually read
9. Finding words for performance-review feedback
10. Turning bullet points into prose and back

### Information Overload
11. Drowning in a long report due tomorrow
12. Prepping for a meeting on a doc you didn't read
13. Pulling action items out of messy notes
14. Comparing two long documents for what changed
15. Decoding dense jargon (legal, medical, technical)
16. Getting the gist of material outside your field
17. Tracking decisions scattered across threads

### Planning & Organization
18. Breaking a vague big task into steps
19. Building a realistic timeline
20. Turning a recurring process into a checklist
21. Drafting a meeting agenda
22. Figuring out what questions to ask before deciding
23. Planning a multi-part event
24. Triaging a chaotic to-do list

### The Blank Page
25. Not knowing how to start
26. Producing an ugly-but-usable first draft
27. Generating ideas when stuck
28. Naming things (projects, products, files) [LOW EVIDENCE — fold into other lessons]
29. Outlining a presentation

### Learning & Understanding
30. Learning new software without the manual
31. Getting a concept explained at your level
32. Re-explaining something a textbook explained badly
33. Studying for a certification
34. Practicing a skill with a patient tutor

### Data Without Code
35. Making sense of a spreadsheet you didn't build
36. Spotting themes in a pile of feedback
37. Sorting/categorizing a long list [FOLD into data lessons]
38. Turning raw numbers into plain English
39. Building a formula without knowing Excel functions [FOLD into data lessons]

### Judgment Support
40. Weighing pros and cons
41. Stress-testing a plan for failure points
42. Getting a second opinion when you work solo
43. Anticipating objections before a pitch

### Personal & Admin
44. Drafting a tricky personal message (apology, negotiation)
45. Understanding a contract or policy you received
46. Rehearsing a difficult conversation
47. Writing a job posting
48. Organizing applicants

## Bucket 2 — Problems people hit when first learning AI (51-100)

### Fear & Emotional Barriers
51. Feeling behind and embarrassed to start
52. Afraid of looking stupid asking basic things
53. Worried using AI is "cheating"
54. Job-loss fear causing paralysis
55. "This isn't for non-technical people like me"
56. Anxiety about doing it wrong
57. Overwhelmed by the number of tools

### Broken Mental Models
58. Not knowing what AI actually is (vs. search or magic)
59. Thinking it "knows" facts like a database
60. Assuming it's always right
61. Not realizing it can be confidently wrong (hallucination)
62. Believing there's one correct way to prompt
63. Confusion about whether it remembers past chats
64. Mixing up tools (ChatGPT vs. Claude vs. Copilot)
65. Assuming it's always (or never) connected to the internet
66. Thinking it has opinions or feelings [LOW EVIDENCE — fold into broader lesson]
67. Confused why the same prompt gives different answers

### Prompting Mistakes
68. One-line vague prompts → vague answers
69. Giving no context about themselves or the goal
70. Not specifying format, length, or tone
71. Giving up after one bad answer
72. Not realizing they can ask follow-ups
73. Cramming everything into one tangled prompt
74. Not knowing how to redirect the AI
75. Pasting output without editing
76. Not showing an example of "good"
77. Asking for things it's bad at (live data, exact math)

### Trust & Verification
78. Not knowing when to trust output
79. Not knowing how to verify a claim
80. Believing fabricated sources
81. Over-trusting on high-stakes calls
82. Under-trusting to the point of not using it
83. Not knowing what AI is bad at

### Privacy & Safety
84. Not knowing what's unsafe to paste
85. Pasting client/patient data into public tools
86. Not understanding where their data goes
87. Fear it's "stealing" their work
88. Unaware of company/industry AI policy

### First-Use Friction
89. Don't know which tool to open first
90. Free vs. paid confusion
91. Intimidated by the empty chat box
92. Don't know what tasks are good fits
93. Can't tell a good answer from a plausible bad one
94. Don't know they can save a prompt that worked
95. Lose good outputs by not saving them
96. Don't know the jargon (token, prompt, model, hallucinate)
97. Get a wall of text, don't know how to ask for less
98. Don't know they can paste in documents
99. Don't know they can assign the AI a role
100. Think they must be polite/formal for it to work

## Bucket 3 — Problems people hit once using AI (101-150)

### Quality Plateaus
101. Output feels generic and "AI-sounding"
102. Can't get it to match their voice
103. Too verbose, too formal, too hedgy
104. Answers stay surface-level
105. It repeats and pads
106. Inconsistent format across uses
107. Subtly wrong in ways that slip through

### Workflow & Integration
108. Using AI ad hoc instead of as a repeatable system
109. Re-typing the same context every time
110. Not chaining steps together
111. No personal prompt library
112. Not integrated into the tools they already use
113. Losing context when switching tools
114. Doing manually what could be templated

### Verification at Scale
115. Checking everything becomes the bottleneck
116. Not knowing which parts to verify vs. trust
117. Catching errors only after shipping
118. No efficient fact-checking process

### Teams & Collaboration
119. Prompts that work for them break for teammates
120. Inconsistent use across a team
121. No shared playbook or standards
122. Teaching others without feeling like a fraud
123. Winning over skeptical colleagues or a boss

### Over-Reliance & Judgment
124. Outsourcing thinking they should keep doing
125. Skill atrophy from leaning too hard
126. Accepting the AI's framing unquestioned
127. Using it to dodge hard decisions instead of supporting them

### Industry-Specific Application
128. Healthcare: safe documentation, handoffs, patient comms
129. Legal: summaries, research first-pass, confidentiality
130. Education: lesson planning, feedback, differentiation
131. Finance: analysis prep, reporting, compliance awareness
132. Operations: SOPs, automation candidates, templates
133. HR: job posts, screening prep, policy drafting
134. Sales/marketing: outreach, repurposing, personalization
135. Exec/admin support: scheduling, briefing, inbox triage

### Advanced Literacy
136. Recognizing when a task is a bad fit for AI
137. Choosing the right tool for the job
138. Understanding cost/speed/quality tradeoffs
139. Spotting bias in output
140. Knowing newer isn't always better
141. Keeping current without chasing every release
142. Catching when the AI is just agreeing with them
143. Designing a prompt someone else can run reliably
144. Building a simple no-code multi-step automation
145. Measuring whether AI is actually saving time

### Ethics & Responsibility
146. Disclosing AI use appropriately
147. Avoiding plagiarism and attribution problems
148. Owning errors they're accountable for
149. Using AI accessibly and inclusively [LOW EVIDENCE — fold into broader lesson]
150. Setting personal limits on what to delegate [LOW EVIDENCE — fold into broader lesson]

## Bucket 4 — Web-validated additions (promoted into curriculum)

### 4A — Authenticity, trust & relationship cost
4A-1. "If I send an AI-written note, people will think I'm lazy or don't care." [STRONG]
4A-2. "My heartfelt message will land as cold and impersonal." [STRONG]
4A-3. "If I admit I used AI, I take a reputational hit — but hiding it feels dishonest." [STRONG]
4A-4. "Customers and audiences are fatigued by anything that smells AI-generated." [MODERATE]
4A-5. "Using AI feels like a secret advantage I have to hide from my boss." [STRONG]

### 4B — AI-detection false positives
4B-6. "My own writing gets flagged as AI when I wrote every word." [STRONG]
4B-7. "Being polished and professional triggers false-positive flags." [STRONG]
4B-8. "As a non-native English speaker, I'm disproportionately flagged." [STRONG]
4B-9. "Teachers/managers treat detector output as proof; I can't defend myself." [MODERATE]

### 4C — Model instability (FOLDED into "keeping current" lesson, not standalone)
4C-10. Tool changed overnight and my workflow stopped working.
4C-11. Same prompt gives different answers on different days.
4C-12. It feels dumber/lazier than it used to.
4C-13. I built my routine around one model and they retired it.
4C-14. Can't keep up with new versions without it becoming a second job.

### 4D — Sycophancy & false confidence
4D-15. "It just agrees with me and validates bad ideas." [STRONG]
4D-16. "It talks me into things or fuels my emotions instead of honest pushback." [MODERATE]
4D-17. "I'm starting to over-trust it for emotional and decision support." [MODERATE]

### 4E — First-timer onboarding (highest priority for non-technical users)
4E-18. "I don't understand AI well enough to even start." [STRONG]
4E-19. "I can't tell free from paid — am I being charged for something free?" [MODERATE]
4E-20. "Signup and account setup are themselves a barrier." [MODERATE]
4E-21. "Nobody taught me; I was handed a license and left to figure it out." [STRONG]
4E-22. "I prompt it like a Google search, get shallow results, conclude it's useless." [STRONG]

### 4F — Tool adoption & workflow-fit failures (FOLDED into "making AI stick" lesson)
4F-23. "I have a Copilot license but it doesn't do what I expected, so I stopped."
4F-24. "Fixing the AI's messy draft takes longer than just doing it myself."
4F-25. "AI sits outside the tools I already use, so I drift back to the old way."

### 4G — Verification fatigue & team coordination (FOLDED into verify/team lessons)
4G-28. "Checking the AI has become its own full-time job."
4G-29. "Teammates and I each ask the AI the same question and get different answers."

### 4H — Profession-specific (Healthcare + Legal in build; others = roadmap)
